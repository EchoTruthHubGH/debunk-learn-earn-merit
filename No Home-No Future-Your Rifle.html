No Home. No Future.
Your Rifle.

When a society systematically dismantles resilience, realism, and identity — who do you actually send to the front?

8 June 2026  |  By Mímir Mímisbrunnr  |  Pan-European

Drawing from the column "Geen woning. Geen toekomst. Maar wél het front." by Max von Kreyfelt (@MaxVonKreyfelt).
Original: x.com/maxvonkreyfelt/status/2063843215325757664
Title from comment by Robert M. Van Praag.

There is something jarring in how politics speaks to young people. They are told to take responsibility, show solidarity, and defend democracy and the fatherland. At the same time, many reach their mid-thirties still competing for a 22m² studio at €1,400 a month — if a door can be found at all. Student debt is routine. Homeownership feels like distant fiction. A stable rental contract has become rarer than a backstage pass at Lowlands.

Opening visual — the lived reality behind the column.

This is the Netherlands in 2026. Yet the same voices that describe housing as "challenging" appear on screen, blood-serious, calling for "resilience," "dienstplicht," "NATO obligations," and readiness to fight for values and territory.

Which territory, precisely? The one where entire neighbourhoods are acquired by pension funds and international investment firms while starters face multi-year waits? The one where a working twenty-something on a median income has less financial room to breathe than their parents did on a single salary in 1987?

The political elite seems genuinely surprised that the emotional bond between younger generations and their institutions is fraying.

The Dutch Case in Numbers

Values without perspective become abstract slogans.

Measured: The Dutch squeeze

  • Prospective buyers under 35 face affordability shortfalls often exceeding €100,000 (NIDI / CBS).
  • Student housing shortages are acute (Kences, 2025).
  • High home prices directly linked to postponed family formation and declining birth rates (NIDI 2026).
  • Conscription discussions have accelerated. Netherlands targeting 122,000 military personnel by 2030.

Questions that travel beyond borders

When societies intensify defence demands while the historical conditions for that readiness are structurally eroded — what does this imply for long-term cohesion?

If housing is treated primarily as an asset class while young adults face elevated cost-overburden, what signal is sent about whose security comes first?
